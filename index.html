1 · The patient does not feel the price

One implant plus one hospital stay is expensive. But in most countries, insurance pays almost all of it. In the US, about 86% of these patients are on Medicare, and Medicare caps what they pay out of pocket.

4 · The price war — try it

Here is the danger. The leader makes ~$12,000 profit per device. If a cheap rival appears, the leader can cut its price and still make money. Your device must survive that.

🎯 To keep a healthy margin (≥50%) even after a 30% price cut by the leader, a challenger's build cost must stay at or below ~$4,500 — and ~$4,000 if the cut reaches 35%. That is a design target, not an afterthought.

5 · Taiwan plays a different game

Taiwan's National Health Insurance pays the hospital a fixed number of points for the device — today about 174,600 points (≈US$5,400) for this category. The hospital keeps the difference between that payment and what it pays the maker.

Two real caveats: (1) by rule, a new device in an existing category is paid the lowest price already in that category — and two rival devices are already listed, at prices we could not yet confirm; (2) approval takes roughly 2–3 years.

⚖️ In public-insurance markets, a cheaper device mostly saves money for the insurer or the hospital — depending on how the price rule is written. Read the rule before writing the business plan.

6 · Four levers — price alone is not enough

The market grew fast (leader's sales: $1.96B in 2025, +29%). Rivals compete on clinical data and easier procedures — nobody competes on price yet. A challenger needs all four levers:

💰 Price

Sell near $10–12k in the US and hospitals flip from loss to profit. Build cost target: ≤ $4,000.

🛡️ Safety

One serious complication adds $10–20k of cost. A safer device protects the hospital's margin — and the patient.

⚡ Simplicity

Shorter procedures, less imaging, less anesthesia = more cases per day. Often worth more to hospitals than the price cut itself.

📋 Reimbursement

Fit the existing billing codes. A device that needs new codes waits years for payment.

🧗 The hardest question is not financial: "Why should a hospital drop a device with 5 years of trial data for yours?" The answer must be clinical evidence — which costs years and tens of millions to produce. Budget for it from day one.
